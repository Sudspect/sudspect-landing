How Pet Amenities Increase Lease Renewals at Apartment Complexes

The Pet Owner Renter Is Your Most Valuable Resident

Consider two prospective residents. The first is a single professional with no pets who can realistically live anywhere. The second is a couple with a dog who needs pet-friendly housing, accessible walking areas, and ideally some on-site pet conveniences. Who is more likely to renew their lease?

The answer, backed by years of multifamily data, is consistently the pet owner — because their cost of moving is higher. Finding a new pet-friendly apartment takes real effort. Paying pet deposits twice is expensive. Disrupting a dog's routine is stressful. Pet-owning renters move less often, and when a property gives them genuine reasons to stay, they stay for years.

According to the American Pet Products Association, approximately 70% of U.S. households own at least one pet. Among renters specifically, survey data consistently puts pet ownership at 72% or higher. The math is simple: the majority of your residents have a pet, or plan to get one. Whether your property serves that demographic well is one of the clearest drivers of renewal rates you can control.

Section 1: The Real Cost of Turnover

Before evaluating any amenity, it helps to understand what you're measuring against. Turnover is expensive — more expensive than most operators account for in day-to-day budgeting.

The National Apartment Association estimates that each unit turnover costs between $3,000 and $5,000 on average, once you factor in:

  • Vacancy loss — the revenue gap between a departing and arriving resident
  • Make-ready costs — cleaning, repainting, minor repairs
  • Leasing costs — advertising, agent commissions or staff time, credit screening
  • Administrative costs — lease processing, utility transfers, move-in coordination

For a 200-unit community with a 50% annual turnover rate, that's 100 turnovers per year and $300,000–$500,000 in churn-related costs. Reducing turnover by even 5–10 percentage points saves $15,000–$50,000 annually.

An amenity investment that retains five additional households per year at an average renewal value of $1,200/month pays for itself many times over in the first year alone — before accounting for revenue share, referrals, or improved property reputation.

Section 2: What Pet Owners Actually Want in a Rental Community

Pet owners do not just want "pets allowed." That bar has been cleared by nearly every apartment community in competitive markets. What they want — and will pay a premium for, and stay longer to keep — is a property that makes daily pet ownership convenient.

When surveyed, pet-owning renters consistently rank the following as their top priorities:

  1. Easy outdoor access — ground-floor or elevator access to dog walking areas without a long trek
  2. On-site waste management — waste bag stations throughout the community
  3. A dedicated space to bathe their dog — this ranks higher than most operators expect
  4. Off-leash exercise space — a dog park or fenced area, even a modest one
  5. A community that feels genuinely pet-welcoming — not grudgingly tolerant

The third item on that list is often underestimated. Residents bathing dogs in their own tubs creates real friction: wet floors, clogged drains, the physical difficulty of holding a large dog in a small tub, and the mess that follows. An on-site dog wash station solves a problem residents experience repeatedly — not once.

Section 3: Dog Wash Stations — Why Frequency Matters

When comparing the impact of different pet amenities on renewal rates, frequency of use is the most reliable predictor of perceived value. A dog park gets used multiple times a day. A pet spa gets used once every six to eight weeks. An on-site dog wash station lands in between — most dogs need bathing every two to four weeks, meaning a resident with a dog may use the station 12–25 times per year.

That frequency builds habit. Residents who use your amenity every few weeks are residents who have incorporated your property into their routine. And residents who have incorporated a property into their routine renew.

The comparison to professional grooming also matters. A full grooming appointment at a salon typically costs $60–$120 and requires scheduling several days in advance. A self-service wash on-site can be done the same day, at $27.99–$34.99, in about 20 minutes. For routine between-appointment maintenance — a muddy dog, a post-beach visit, seasonal shedding — the on-site station wins every time.

That consistent, tangible value is what residents cite in satisfaction surveys and mention to friends considering your community.

Section 4: The Revenue Share Model — An Amenity That Pays You Back

The traditional barrier to adding amenities is cost. Capital expenditures compete with deferred maintenance, renovation budgets, and distribution targets. For many operators, even a clearly beneficial amenity gets delayed because the upfront investment doesn't clear the hurdle rate.

Revenue share amenity programs change that equation.

Companies like Sudspect — a veteran-owned Florida business — place fully operational, AI-powered dog wash machines inside apartment communities and HOA developments at zero cost to the property. Sudspect handles installation, maintenance, cleaning, and repairs. The property provides the space and earns a percentage of every wash completed on-site.

Depending on contract structure, properties earn between 5% and 20% of gross wash revenue. At a community where residents complete 200 washes per quarter at an average price of $31 per wash, that generates:

  • At 5%: approximately $945 per quarter
  • At 10%: approximately $1,890 per quarter
  • At 15%: approximately $2,835 per quarter
  • At 20%: approximately $3,780 per quarter

This model flips the amenity conversation. Instead of asking "what does this cost us?", the question becomes "how much will this earn us?" — while simultaneously improving resident satisfaction and supporting lease renewal rates.

The Compounding Effect

Pet amenities do not operate in isolation. A community that installs a dog wash station, maintains waste stations, and creates even a modest off-leash area signals to current and prospective residents that pet ownership is genuinely welcome here. That signal:

  • Attracts higher-quality applicants who intend to stay long-term
  • Differentiates the property in online searches and review platforms
  • Generates word-of-mouth referrals from satisfied pet-owning residents
  • Supports premium rent positioning in markets where pet-friendly housing is competitive

None of these effects require a large budget to initiate. The highest-impact move for many properties is to start with a zero-cost amenity that immediately serves residents and generates revenue — then build from there.
