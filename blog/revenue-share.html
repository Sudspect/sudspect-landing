Revenue Share Amenity Programs: How Florida Properties Earn Passive Income

The Zero-Cost Amenity Model Is Changing How Properties Think About Upgrades

For most of multifamily real estate history, adding an amenity meant one thing: spending money. Capital budgets had to be secured, installation contracts signed, maintenance staff trained, and equipment eventually replaced. The question was never really "should we add this?" — it was "can we justify the cost?"

A new category of amenity partnerships has changed that calculus. In a revenue share amenity model, a third-party provider installs and operates equipment on your property at no cost to you. The provider keeps the majority of the revenue generated; the property receives a percentage — typically without lifting a finger. The property gets a genuine amenity that improves resident experience. The provider gets access to a captive user base. Both parties benefit.

These arrangements have existed in vending machines and laundry equipment for decades. What's newer is the sophistication of what's being placed — and the resident value being delivered. AI-powered dog wash machines, smart package lockers, EV charging stations, and automated fitness equipment are all finding their way into apartment communities under zero-cost or revenue-share terms.

For Florida property owners, HOA boards, and asset managers looking to improve Net Operating Income without drawing from capital reserves, understanding how these programs work — and what separates a good partner from a problematic one — is increasingly important.

Section 1: How Revenue Share Works in Practice

The mechanics are straightforward. A provider approaches a property, proposes placing a piece of equipment in an available space (a utility room, a common area alcove, a covered outdoor area), and offers the property a cut of everything the equipment earns.

The provider covers:

  • Equipment purchase or lease
  • Installation, including any plumbing, electrical, or structural requirements
  • Ongoing maintenance and repairs
  • Cleaning or sanitization
  • Customer support for residents

The property contributes:

  • Space allocation (typically 50–200 square feet depending on the equipment)
  • Access to residents and any reasonable utility connections

Revenue is tracked automatically, often through app-based or cloud-connected equipment, and the property's share is typically distributed monthly or quarterly.

The revenue percentage varies by contract. Longer-term commitments and higher-volume locations typically command better terms for the property — anywhere from 5% to 20% is common depending on the category and partner.

Section 2: What to Look For in a Revenue Share Partner

Not all programs are created equal. Before signing a placement agreement, property managers and HOA boards should evaluate the following:

Who owns the equipment?
Ownership matters for liability and end-of-contract terms. Equipment should remain the provider's responsibility throughout the agreement. Avoid arrangements where ownership transfers to you by default at contract end — that can leave you holding outdated machinery.

Who handles maintenance?
This is the most important practical question. The provider should be contractually responsible for all maintenance, repairs, and replacements. If the machine breaks down, the call should go to them — not to your maintenance team. Any agreement that pushes repair responsibility back to the property after a warranty period undermines the zero-cost premise.

How is revenue tracked and reported?
Modern equipment uses connected software to track every transaction in real time. You should receive clear, verifiable monthly statements — not just a check with no documentation. Transparency builds trust and makes it easy to project future earnings.

What happens if the relationship isn't working?
Review contract exit provisions carefully. Reasonable agreements include termination clauses that allow either party to exit with appropriate notice — typically 30–90 days — without penalty after the initial term. Be cautious about multi-year commitments with aggressive early termination fees if the program underperforms.

Does the provider carry liability insurance?
Equipment on your property creates potential liability exposure. The provider should carry commercial general liability insurance naming your property as an additional insured. Ask for a certificate before installation.

Section 3: Why Dog Wash Machines Work as a Revenue Share Amenity

Not all equipment categories are equally well-suited to the revenue share model. The best candidates share a few characteristics: high resident demand, high utilization frequency, low physical footprint, and no ongoing labor requirement from property staff. Dog wash machines check all four boxes.

High resident demand: Approximately 72% of U.S. renters own pets. In Florida, where outdoor lifestyle and year-round dog activity is the norm, that number tends to skew even higher. On-site dog washing solves a real, recurring problem for a large share of your residents.

High utilization frequency: Dogs get dirty regularly. Unlike a pet spa that residents might visit every six to eight weeks, a self-service wash station gets used every one to three weeks per pet-owning household. Higher frequency means more transactions, more consistent revenue, and a stronger case for residents to renew their lease to keep the amenity.

Low physical footprint: A professional-grade dog wash machine fits comfortably in a 60–80 square foot space. That's a utility closet, a converted storage room, or a corner of a pet-friendly common area. Many properties find space that was previously underutilized or generating no revenue at all.

No staff required: Autonomous machines manage the full wash cycle, sanitize between uses, and process payments without any involvement from property staff. There are no scheduling requirements, no training needs, and no additional payroll.

Total quarterly gross revenue from the machine: approximately $6,200.
